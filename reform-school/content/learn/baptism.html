Baptism is not a ritual of membership — it is a declaration of death and resurrection. By immersion in water, we publicly confess our faith in Christ's death and resurrection, testify to our own death to sin, and declare our intention to walk in newness of life. It is a line crossed, a covenant made, a life begun.

A Symbol of Union with Christ

The water of baptism speaks of burial and resurrection. To go under is to die with Christ — to leave behind the old self, the old allegiances, the old life. To come up is to rise with him — new, forgiven, adopted, and indwelt by the Spirit. Baptism does not save us, but it marks the moment we publicly declared that Christ has.

Romans 6:4 "We were therefore buried with him through baptism into death in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, we too may live a new life."

By Immersion

The New Testament practice of baptism was by full immersion — and for good reason. Only immersion fully pictures the burial and resurrection that baptism symbolises. It is not a casual sprinkling but a whole-body, all-in commitment to a life surrendered to Christ. The manner of baptism carries the message of baptism.

Acts 2:38 "Peter replied, 'Repent and be baptised, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit.'"

A Public Confession

Baptism is inherently public. It is the moment the private decision of faith becomes a public confession. It follows repentance, instruction in Scripture, and an affirmation of faith — and it receives us formally as members of Christ's church, welcomed into the community of those who are walking the same road.

Matthew 28:19 "Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptising them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit."

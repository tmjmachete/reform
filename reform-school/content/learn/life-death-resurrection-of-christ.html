Everything depends on what happened in three days outside Jerusalem. The life Christ lived, the death He died, and the resurrection that followed are not isolated events — they are the hinge of history, the ground of our salvation, and the guarantee of our future.

A Life of Perfect Obedience

Jesus did not simply come to die. He came to live — and to live the life we should have lived. Every temptation he resisted, every commandment he kept, every act of love he performed was an offering to the Father on our behalf. His righteousness is not merely an example to follow. It is a gift to receive.

Romans 5:19 "For just as through the disobedience of the one man the many were made sinners, so also through the obedience of the one man the many will be made righteous."

A Death That Saves

The cross is not a tragedy — it is a triumph. Christ's death was substitutionary: He took the penalty that was ours. It was expiatory: it removed the guilt of our sin. It was reconciling: it brought us back to God. And it was transforming: it changes everything about how we live. On the cross, justice and mercy embraced.

2 Corinthians 5:21 "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."

A Resurrection That Changes Everything

If Christ had remained in the tomb, faith would be futile. But he rose — bodily, historically, undeniably. His resurrection declares God's victory over sin and death, guarantees the resurrection of all who trust in him, and proclaims that death is not the final word. The story is not over. It has only just begun.

1 Corinthians 15:20 "But Christ has indeed been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep."

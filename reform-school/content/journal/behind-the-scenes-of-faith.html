Social media has given us a very particular picture of what walking with God looks and feels like. I am not against that. But when you pull too hard in one direction, reality has a way of hitting differently.

We all love a worship session where you pour your heart out. We all love a sermon that stays with you. We all love a Bible study that leaves your mind lit up with something new. But how many of us talk about what happens behind the scenes?

The self-doubt. The imposter syndrome. The hollow feeling when you slip back into old patterns.

Every Sabbath, statuses flood with experiences of an amazing day. Beautiful moments — and they are real. But no one usually talks about what comes after. It is a Sunday afternoon and you have fallen into old habits and sinned again. You have missed your devotion countless times through the week and you feel like you are losing God. The moment where the excitement fades and you are left with just yourself — and you are not sure that is enough.

This is the picture we rarely project. And yet, this is where most of us actually live.

Character Building

Character Building

One of the most sobering passages in my favourite chapter in Ministry of Healing — “The Importance of Seeking True Knowledge” — reads:

Ministry of Healing, p. 452.1

“Wrongs cannot be righted, nor can reformations in conduct be made by a few feeble, intermittent efforts. Character building is the work, not of a day, nor of a year, but of a lifetime.”

And then, even more sharply:

Ministry of Healing, p. 452.2

“In one moment, by a hasty, unguarded act, we may place ourselves in the power of evil; but it requires more than a moment to break the fetters and attain to a holier life.”

It often feels like an endless pursuit. But this is where the real work lives.

The Individual Walk

The Individual Walk

And this work is deeply personal. No one can carry it for you.

Ministry of Healing, p. 453.3

“No one will be borne upward without stern, persevering effort in his own behalf. All must engage in this warfare for themselves; no one else can fight our battles. Individually we are responsible for the issues of the struggle; though Noah, Job, and Daniel were in the land they could deliver neither son nor daughter by their righteousness.”

There is a quiet temptation to outsource this. To lean on the church programme. To let the organisation carry what only you can carry.

Last Day Events, p. 75.2

“Everywhere there is a tendency to substitute the work of organizations for individual effort.”

Last Day Events, p. 76.1

“Christ commits to His followers an individual work — a work that cannot be done by proxy.”

“Your growth is not a group project.”

The Closing Truth

The Closing Truth

This unglamorous work leads to results more lasting than anything glamorous. That which only appeals to emotion, without engaging the mind, may not produce the results we are hoping for.

So what is the result I am actually pointing to?

Ministry of Healing, p. 470.1

“The strongest argument in favor of the gospel is a loving and lovable Christian.”

That is it. Not a highlight reel. Not a perfect testimony. Not an unbroken streak of quiet times and Sunday morning energy. Just us — becoming loving and lovable — and through that, pointing souls to Christ.

The behind-the-scenes work is the work. And it is worth it.

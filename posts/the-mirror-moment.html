Issue No. 01  ·  The Beginning

The Mirror
Moment

What happens when you stop, look honestly at yourself, and decide you are tired of what you see.

A journal entry for the journey  ·  re:form

There is a moment — maybe at 2am, maybe in a quiet room, maybe right in the middle of a busy day — when you catch yourself. And you do not quite like what you find.

Not the version of yourself you show people. Not the one that smiles in photos or says the right things at the right times. The other one. The one that keeps doing what it promised it would stop doing. The one that thinks things you would never say out loud. The one that feels — if you are really honest — like it is losing.

I know that feeling. And if you have ever felt it too, I want you to know: you are not alone. And more importantly — that feeling is not the end of something. It might actually be the beginning.

The Honest Mirror

There was a man — brilliant, passionate, deeply committed to God — who sat down and wrote something that surprised everyone who read it. His name was Paul. And after everything he had seen and done and believed, this is what came out of him:

Romans 7:24
"O wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death?"

Not enough. Broken. Flawed. These are the words we carry when we finally stop pretending.

That is not a man who has given up on God. That is a man who has looked in the mirror — really looked — and told the truth about what he saw. There is something almost sacred about that kind of honesty.

Steps to Christ, p. 37
"The confession that is the outpouring of the inmost soul finds its way to the God of infinite pity. The Lord is nigh unto them that are of a broken heart; and saveth such as be of a contrite spirit."

Most of us spend a lot of energy avoiding that mirror. We stay busy. We fill the silence. We compare ourselves to people who seem to be doing worse and use that as comfort. But the mirror has a way of finding us eventually. And when it does — when we are finally broken enough to be honest — God is not far away. He is right there.

Why Are We Like This?

So what is actually going on? Why do we keep doing the things we do not want to do? Why does the world feel so heavy, so broken, so full of pain — including the pain we cause each other and ourselves?

Romans 3:23
"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."

That word — sin — gets used a lot in religious spaces and sometimes it loses its weight. But strip away the religious language for a moment. What Paul is describing is this: something inside us is broken. Not just our behaviour. Our nature. And Paul describes it from the inside with startling honesty:

Romans 7:15, 17
"I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do... it is sin living in me."

If you have ever said, I keep promising myself I will change, and I keep not changing — that is exactly what Paul is describing. It is not weakness. It is not stupidity. It is a condition that goes deeper than willpower can reach.

Steps to Christ, p. 23
"How shall a man be just with God? How shall the sinner be made righteous? It is only through Christ that we can be brought into harmony with God, with holiness; but how are we to come to Christ?"

That question is the most important one any of us will ever ask. And the answer is simpler than most people expect.

The Turning Point

Here is where things start to shift. In Romans 7:18, Paul says something that sounds like defeat but is actually the beginning of freedom: nothing good lives in me on my own. I have tried. I cannot fix this from the inside.

The moment of desperation is not a bad thing. It is the moment we stop running and start reaching.

That exhaustion — the kind that makes you finally stop pretending — is often the most honest prayer a person ever prays. It sounds like: I cannot do this anymore. I need help.

At the Feet of Jesus

In John 8, there is a woman who had no time to prepare herself. She was dragged — publicly, humiliatingly — in front of a crowd and placed before Jesus. Fully exposed. No defence. No clean record. Nothing to offer.

Steps to Christ, p. 26
"They think that they cannot come to Christ unless they first repent, and that repentance prepares for the forgiveness of their sins... But must the sinner wait till he has repented before he can come to Jesus? Is repentance to be made an obstacle between the sinner and the Saviour? The Bible does not teach that the sinner must repent before he can heed the invitation of Christ, 'Come unto Me, all ye that labor and are heavy-laden, and I will give you rest.' It is the virtue that goes forth from Christ, that leads to genuine repentance. We can no more repent without the Spirit of Christ to awaken the conscience than we can be pardoned without Christ."

You do not have to be ready. You do not have to be clean. You do not have to have repented first. The very desire to come — that reaching, that desperation — is already Christ drawing you. So come as you are.

But here is what needs to be said clearly: God is not calling you to stay as you are. He is calling you as you are — because that is the only version of you that exists right now — so that He can begin the work of changing who you are. The invitation and the transformation come from the same hand.

Jesus looked at the woman, and after the crowd had gone, he said:

John 8:11
"Neither do I condemn you. Go and sin no more."

Both parts matter. Neither do I condemn you — that is grace receiving her exactly where she is. Go and sin no more — that is grace commissioning her toward something new. He did not receive her in order to leave her the same. He received her so that she could become different.

Come and Talk
Isaiah 1:18
"Come now, let us reason together, says the Lord. Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow."

Notice what God says. Come and reason with me. Not from a distance. Not after you have sorted yourself out. He is pulling us close — into an embrace — and it is from that place of closeness that the conversation happens. We do not reason with God from afar. We reason with him in his arms.

Psalm 119:9
"How can a young person stay on the path of purity? By living according to your word."

We do not walk clean by trying harder. We walk clean by walking with the Word. That is what this journal is about. That is what re:form has always been about. Finding our way back — together, one honest step at a time.

Where We Are Headed

And here is the beautiful thing about all of this. God is not waiting for you to climb a mountain or perform something extraordinary to earn your way back. The conditions of coming to him are simpler than religion often makes them sound:

Steps to Christ, p. 37
"The conditions of obtaining mercy of God are simple and just and reasonable. The Lord does not require us to do some grievous thing in order that we may have the forgiveness of sin. We need not make long and wearisome pilgrimages, or perform painful penances... but he that confesseth and forsaketh his sin shall have mercy."

Confess. Forsake. Receive mercy. That is the whole invitation. And the one who extends that invitation is the same one who holds the power to make you new.

God is not calling you to stay as you are. He is calling you as you are — because that is the only version of you that exists right now — so that He can begin the work of changing who you are. The invitation and the transformation come from the same hand.

The Prayer of the Journey
"Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me."
Psalm 51:10

Not fix my behaviour. Not help me try harder. Create. Start something entirely new. From the inside out. That is what we are after. And I believe God does exactly that — for anyone who comes and asks.

This is where we reason with God. Not from a distance — but here, held close, in his arms.
Isaiah 1:18
